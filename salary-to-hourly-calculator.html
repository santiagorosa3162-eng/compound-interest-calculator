Why Convert Salary to Hourly?

Converting between annual salary and hourly wage helps you compare job offers that are quoted differently, evaluate whether a freelance or consulting rate is competitive, and understand your true hourly earnings so you can make informed decisions about your time and career.

Many salaried workers underestimate their effective hourly rate when they factor in unpaid overtime. If your $75,000 salary requires you to actually work 50 hours per week instead of 40, your true hourly rate is $28.85 rather than $36.06 — a significant difference that affects how you should value your time and compare with hourly alternatives.

This calculator also helps when deciding between full-time employment and freelancing. Freelance rates need to be higher than equivalent employee rates to account for self-employment taxes, lack of benefits, unpaid administrative time, and income variability. A freelancer typically needs to charge 1.5–2× their equivalent employee hourly rate to match total compensation.

Salary Conversion Formula

The salary to hourly conversion is straightforward:

Hourly Rate = Annual SalaryHours per Week × Weeks per Year
  • Annual Salary — Your total pre-tax annual compensation.
  • Hours per Week — Typical hours worked per week (default: 40).
  • Weeks per Year — Usually 52; use 50 if you take 2 weeks of unpaid leave.
  • Daily Rate — Hourly Rate × Hours per Day (default: 8).
  • Monthly Rate — Annual Salary ÷ 12.

Note: this calculator uses gross (pre-tax) salary. Your take-home hourly rate after federal and state taxes will be significantly lower — typically 25–40% less depending on your bracket and location. Use a take-home pay calculator to find your net hourly rate after taxes.

A quick mental math shortcut: divide your annual salary by 2,000 (40 hours × 50 weeks) to get an approximate hourly rate. So $80,000 ÷ 2,000 = $40/hour. The exact calculation using 2,080 hours (40 × 52) gives $38.46, but the $40 estimate is close enough for quick comparisons.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many working hours are in a year?

A standard full-time year is typically 2,080 hours (40 hours/week × 52 weeks). However, this doesn't account for paid time off. If you take 2 weeks of vacation, the actual hours worked drop to 2,000 (40 × 50). The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics uses 2,080 as the standard. Some employers define full-time as 37.5 hours per week, giving 1,950 hours annually. When calculating a salary offer, always clarify the expected weekly hours to get an accurate hourly equivalent.

How do I set freelance rates based on my salary?

Convert your target annual income to an hourly rate, then apply a freelance multiplier of at least 1.5–2×. Here's why: as a freelancer you pay self-employment tax (15.3%), must fund your own benefits (health insurance, retirement), spend unpaid time on admin, marketing, and business development, and face income gaps between clients. A common formula: (Target income ÷ billable hours) × 1.5 or higher. Many freelancers target 1,000–1,500 billable hours per year rather than 2,080, further increasing the required rate. Research market rates for your skill set to validate your calculation.

Is salary or hourly pay better?

It depends on the role and your situation. Salary provides income stability and often comes with more benefits. Hourly pay compensates you for every hour worked, including overtime (often at 1.5×). If your salaried role regularly requires 50–60 hours per week, your effective hourly rate is much lower than nominal, while an hourly worker earns proportionally more in that situation. Salaried roles often (but not always) come with more career advancement, benefits, and job security. Non-exempt hourly workers are entitled to overtime protections under the FLSA that salary workers may not have.

What is a good hourly rate or annual salary?

The U.S. median household income was approximately $74,580 in 2022, equating to about $35.86/hour for a standard full-time year. "Good" varies enormously by location, industry, experience, and education. A $50,000 salary in rural Mississippi and a $50,000 salary in San Francisco represent very different standards of living. The most useful benchmark is your local cost of living: calculate the income needed to cover housing, transportation, food, and other essentials in your area, then determine how much above that minimum you earn — that's your real financial position regardless of nominal salary.
